💨 Deep Breathing Exercises Parasympathetic activators
Box Breathing (4-4-4-4) 4 min
1.Sit upright. Exhale fully to empty the lungs first.
2.Inhale slowly through your nose — count 4 seconds.
3.Hold at the top — count 4 seconds.
4.Exhale slowly through your mouth — count 4 seconds.
5.Hold empty — count 4 seconds. Repeat 4–6 cycles.
Best for: pre-meeting reset, midday recalibration. Used by Navy SEALs under acute stress — and that's exactly what your HR role generates.

4-7-8 Breathing 3 min
1.Exhale completely to start.
2.Inhale through nose — 4 counts.
3.Hold breath — 7 counts.
4.Exhale through mouth with a whoosh — 8 counts.
5.That's one cycle. Do 4 cycles only — this is potent.
Best for: panic onset, before sleep, post-lashing out. The extended exhale activates the vagus nerve — direct brake on your stress response.

Diaphragmatic (Belly) Breathing 5 min
1.Lie down or sit reclined. Place one hand on chest, one on belly.
2.Breathe in slowly — only your belly hand should rise. Chest stays still.
3.Exhale slowly — belly falls back. 6–8 breaths per minute is the target.
4.Continue for 5 minutes. This is the foundational breath for PMR too.
Best for: evening wind-down, general anxiety regulation. Chest breathing (shallow) keeps the sympathetic system active. Belly breathing switches you over.

🧘 Progressive Muscle Relaxation 10–15 min · Before sleep
PMR trains your body to recognise and release tension before it builds into an episode. Do this every night for 2 weeks and your resting muscle tension — and baseline anxiety — will drop measurably.
Full Body PMR Sequence 12 min
→Start: Lie flat on your back. Take 3 diaphragmatic breaths to begin.
1.Feet: Curl your toes tightly — hold 5 seconds — release. Feel the difference.
2.Calves: Point feet toward you, tense calves — hold 5 sec — release.
3.Thighs: Tighten thigh muscles — hold 5 sec — release.
4.Glutes: Squeeze — hold 5 sec — release.
5.Abdomen: Tighten core like bracing for impact — hold 5 sec — release.
6.Hands: Clench fists tightly — hold 5 sec — release.
7.Arms: Flex biceps — hold 5 sec — release.
8.Shoulders: Shrug toward ears — hold 5 sec — release. This one matters most for HR professionals.
9.Face: Scrunch all facial muscles — hold 5 sec — release. Jaw, forehead, eyes.
→End: Scan body for any remaining tension. Take 3 slow breaths. You're done.

5-4-3-2-1 Grounding (Panic First-Aid) 2 min
5.Name 5 things you can see right now.
4.Name 4 things you can touch/feel (floor, chair, shirt texture).
3.Name 3 things you can hear.
2.Name 2 things you can smell (or remember a smell).
1.Name 1 thing you can taste.
Use during panic onset. It interrupts the runaway thought loop by forcing sensory engagement. "No one dies from panic attacks" — your own note — this is what makes it survivable in the moment.
Midday Body Scan 5 min
1.Close eyes. Start at the top of your head. Move attention slowly down.
2.Notice, don't fix. Tight jaw? Just note it. Tense shoulders? Just name it.
3.Where you find tension — breathe into it. Imagine the breath releasing it on exhale.
4.Move from head → neck → shoulders → chest → belly → arms → legs → feet.
5.Open eyes slowly. Notice if the room feels different.

Thought Observation (Non-Judgmental) 5 min
1.Sit quietly. Let thoughts arise without following them.
2.Imagine each thought as a cloud passing across the sky. You are the sky.
3.When you notice you've followed a thought — gently return. No self-criticism.
4.Especially for cyclothymic patterns: this practice helps you observe mood shifts without being taken over by them.
The goal isn't an empty mind — it's an observing mind. A charismatic leader who can observe their own mood cycle without reacting is formidable.
